Kokborok, a Sino-Tibetan language, has six basic vowel phonemes: /i/, /u/, /e/,/ɘ/, /o/, and /a/. In the Hachukma writing system, graphemes that differ from their phonemic pronunciation are indicated in angle brackets.

The consonant system includes voiceless, aspirated, and voiced stops such as p, t, k, ch, ph, th, kh, b, d, j, and g. It also includes fricatives like s and h, nasals m, n, and ng , the lateral l, the rhotic r, and the approximants w and y. Based on this phonological system, the Hachukma script functions as a fully alphabetic writing system, with independent vowels and consonants. View IPA Chart 🔊

Extra Note on Sound Categories: In the vowel chart, the terms “Front,” “Central,” and “Back” refer to the position of the tongue during pronunciation rather than the letters themselves. “Front” vowels are pronounced with the tongue forward in the mouth, “Central” vowels with the tongue in the middle and “Back” vowels with the tongue pulled toward the back. In the consonant chart, headings like Labial, Alveolar, Lamino-Postalveolar, Velar, and Glottal indicate where in the vocal tract the sound is produced, while rows such as Stops, Fricatives, Nasals, Lateral, Rhotic, and Approximants describe how the airflow is modified during articulation.

Hachukma Script

The Hachukma Object Script is a distinctive pictographic writing system based primarily on the acrophonic principle in which each symbol derives its phonetic value from the initial sound of the object it visually represents. A small number of characters are treated as exceptions, whose phonetic values do not follow this initial-sound pattern. The characters are inspired by culturally significant Tripuri artifacts, human body parts, selected arthropods, and elements of the natural environment, preserving their recognizable forms and maintaining a strong connection to Borok cultural heritage. Although pictorial in origin, the symbols function purely as phonetic signs; in actual use, each letter represents only its assigned sound, rendering the original object meaning semantically inactive. Through this structure, the script operates as a fully alphabetic system while retaining a distinct visual and cultural identity.

Script Type

Hachukma is a fully phonemic alphabet in function. Each character is derived from a pictographic source and represents exactly one sound, with vowels and consonants written as independent symbols. While the characters maintain their visual connection to their original objects, they function solely as phonemic units in reading and writing.

Numerals

The hachukma number system is a base-10 number system, similar to other Sino-Tibeto-Burman numbering systems. It uses its own set of numerals to represent numbers from zero to nine.

Essential Latin keys

On the hachukma QWERTY keyboard, the Latin keys are reassigned to represent essential phonemes: V = Kh, Q = Th, F = Ph, X = NG, Z = ɘ and C = Ch. These keys are placed near the A key to allow faster typing of common syllables such as Pha, Cha, Tha and kha.

Linguistic Concepts

Phonetics is the scientific study of speech sounds, encompassing their production, acoustic properties and perception by the human auditory system. It examines how speech sounds are articulated using the vocal organs, how they are transmitted as sound waves, and how they are perceived and interpreted by listeners.

Examples: /k/ as in "cat," /b/ as in "bat"

A phoneme is the smallest contrastive unit of sound in a language that can distinguish meaning between words. Phonemes are abstract mental sound categories rather than specific physical sounds.

Examples: /p/ and /b/ distinguish “pat” from “bat.”

A syllable is a fundamental unit of speech that typically contains a vowel sound and may include surrounding consonants. Syllables shape rhythm, stress patterns, and pronunciation.

Examples: "cat" (1 syllable), "table" (2 syllables: ta-ble)

A diphthong is a complex vowel sound formed by gliding from one vowel position to another within the same syllable.

Examples: "coin" [oi], "loud" [au]

A consonant cluster is a sequence of two or more consonant sounds occurring together without intervening vowels within a syllable.

Examples: "bl" in "black," "st" in "stop"

Orthography refers to the standardized system of writing in a language, including spelling, capitalization and punctuation rules.

Examples: English spelling conventions.

Acrophonic refers to a principle where a symbol represents the initial sound of the word it originally depicted.

Example: “A” for “Apple.”

A pictographic writing system uses symbols visually derived from recognizable objects or elements of the natural world.

Examples: Early Egyptian hieroglyphs, early Chinese characters.
